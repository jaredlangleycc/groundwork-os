# Positioning Formula Deep Dive

Twelve worked examples of the 5-part positioning formula across different industries. For each, the bad version shows what most founders write before the skill runs. The good version shows what a locked positioning statement looks like.

The formula:

```
[I/We] help [specific ICP]
[achieve specific outcome]
by [specific approach],
unlike [common alternative]
which [shortcoming].
```

---

## Example 1: B2B SaaS coaching (the example coaching business)

**Bad version:**
> "I help leaders grow through transformative coaching."

**Good version:**
> "I help frontline sales managers in their first 18 months at B2B SaaS companies (50-500 employees) hit their team quota and their own number through a 90-day coaching engagement with weekly 1:1s, unlike traditional executive coaching that runs 12+ months at C-suite price points and treats family and career as separate problems."

**What changed:**
- ICP went from "leaders" to "frontline sales managers in first 18 months at B2B SaaS 50-500"
- Outcome went from "grow" to "hit team quota and their own number"
- Approach went from "transformative coaching" to "90-day engagement with weekly 1:1s"
- Alternative went from unstated to "traditional executive coaching"
- Shortcoming went from unstated to "12+ months, C-suite prices, separates family and career"

## Example 2: Fractional CMO

**Bad version:**
> "We help B2B SaaS companies scale revenue through data-driven marketing."

**Good version:**
> "We help B2B SaaS founders under 50 employees fix their broken marketing in 90 days through embedded fractional CMO work, with shipped campaigns and trained junior staff at the end, unlike traditional marketing agencies that sell hours without owning outcomes or training your team."

**What changed:**
- ICP added specificity ("under 50 employees")
- Outcome went from "scale revenue" to "fix broken marketing in 90 days, with shipped campaigns and trained junior staff"
- Approach got specific time bound ("embedded fractional CMO work")
- Alternative named ("traditional marketing agencies")
- Shortcoming honest ("sell hours without owning outcomes")

## Example 3: Web design agency (small business focus)

**Bad version:**
> "We design beautiful, conversion-optimized websites for small businesses."

**Good version:**
> "We help service-based small businesses ($500K to $5M revenue) replace their outdated website with a conversion-tested redesign in 6 weeks, including 90 days of post-launch optimization based on real visitor data, unlike Wix/Squarespace templates that look polished on day one but never improve after launch."

- Outcome got specific ("replace outdated website in 6 weeks plus 90 days of optimization")
- Approach got time-bound
- Alternative named (Wix/Squarespace templates)
- Shortcoming honest ("never improve after launch")

## Example 4: Solo financial planner

**Bad version:**
> "I help clients achieve their financial goals through personalized planning."

**Good version:**
> "I help dual-income tech families with $500K to $5M in invested assets build a 10-year wealth plan that survives stock-grant volatility, through quarterly planning sessions and unlimited email access, unlike robo-advisors that ignore your equity comp structure and traditional wealth managers who require $10M minimums."

**What changed:**
- ICP got specific (dual-income tech families with specific asset range)
- Outcome got time-bound and specific (10-year wealth plan, stock-grant volatility)
- Approach got specific cadence (quarterly + email)
- Two alternatives named (robo-advisors AND traditional wealth managers)
- Shortcomings honest and specific

## Patterns across all 12 examples

### What good positioning shares

1. **ICP includes 3-4 qualifiers.** Stage + size + role + situation. Never just one variable.
2. **Outcome is measurable.** A number, a percentage, a time bound. Not "growth" or "results."
3. **Approach is operationally specific.** Time-bound, cadence-specific, scope-bound.
4. **Alternative is named.** A specific category or specific competitor, not "other options."
5. **Shortcoming is honest.** Not strawman bashing. A specific gap.

### What bad positioning shares

1. **ICP is a market.** "Small business owners," "founders," "leaders," "professionals."
2. **Outcome is aspirational mush.** "Growth," "transformation," "success," "results."
3. **Approach is signaling.** "Transformative," "proprietary," "holistic," "bespoke," "custom."
4. **Alternative is unstated.** No competitor named. No "doing nothing" option named.
5. **Shortcoming is strawman.** "Other options are bad. We are good."

### The test of differentiation

After writing positioning, ask: could any of my top 3 competitors say the same statement?

If yes, the positioning is undifferentiated. Tighten until at least one of the 5 parts becomes specific to YOU.

If no, the positioning is operational. Move forward.

## Domain-specific notes

### Coaching / consulting

The hardest part is usually Part 2 (the outcome). Coaches often want to claim transformation. Push for measurable, time-bound outcomes the buyer would describe to a peer.

### Services / agencies

The hardest part is usually Part 5 (the shortcoming). Agencies do not want to disparage other agencies. The fix is to focus on a specific structural gap (no in-house training, no testing, no outcome ownership) rather than slamming the competitor's quality.

### Solo professionals (therapists, planners, lawyers)

The hardest part is usually Part 3 (the approach). Solo professionals want to keep flexibility. Push for at least the cadence and channel ("weekly 50-min in-person sessions" not "ongoing therapeutic support").

### Course creators / educators

The hardest part is usually Part 4 (the alternative). Course creators want to compare against generic "self-study" or "DIY." Push for a specific competing course or category (e.g., "self-paced Udemy courses" not "online courses").

### Product founders (SaaS, physical products)

The 5-part formula adapts. The "approach" becomes "the product" and the "shortcoming" becomes the legacy alternative's UX or business model gap.

```
PART 4: Alternative
Vague version: [what user said first]
Specific version: [refined after pushback]
LOCKED: [final phrase]

PART 5: Shortcoming
Vague version: [what user said first]
Specific version: [refined after pushback]
Strawman? [no, please]
LOCKED: [final phrase]

COMPOSED STATEMENT:
[I/We] help [Part 1] [Part 2] by [Part 3], unlike [Part 4] which [Part 5].

TESTS:
- Right buyer self-selects in? [predicted: yes/no]
- Wrong buyer self-selects out? [predicted: yes/no]
- Every word load-bearing? [yes/no]
- Competitor could say the same? [yes/no: should be no]
```

If all tests pass, save to `docs/positioning.md` as v1.0. If any test fails, revisit the specific Part.
