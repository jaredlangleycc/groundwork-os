# ICP Discovery

The skill that turns a vague "small business owners" into a precise "B2B SaaS sales managers, under 18 months in role, 50-500 employees, who own a number but did not own one before." Without that precision, positioning is vague, copy is vague, outreach is vague, and the user spends hours per week talking to fits they will never close.

This skill works whether you have zero customers or 50. The depth changes with stage. The discipline does not.

NOT for: positioning statement work (use `positioning`). NOT for: pricing or packaging (use `pricing-strategy`, `offer-design`). NOT for: building a prospect list at volume (use `outreach-drafter` after ICP locks).

## What an ICP is and is not

An ICP is the precise definition of who you sell to. It is more specific than a target market. It is the entry where a sales motion, a pricing decision, a copy decision, or an offer change can be validated.

An ICP is:

- One specific stage of company (e.g., "Series A to B, 50-500 employees, B2B SaaS")
- One specific role (e.g., "Sales Manager, not VP of Sales, not Director")
- One specific situation (e.g., "under 18 months in the role, first-time manager")
- One specific pain (e.g., "missed quota two quarters in a row, blamed reps, knows the issue is them")
- One specific buying trigger (e.g., "boss said get coaching or get replaced")

An ICP is NOT:

- A target market ("B2B SaaS companies" is a market; "Sales Managers <18mo at B2B SaaS 50-500" is an ICP)
- A persona built from imagination ("Sarah is a busy mom and a hard worker" is not an ICP)
- A list of people who might buy (this is a TAM exercise)
- Three different customer types (you cannot serve three ICPs at once until $25K+ MRR)

The test: can the user name 5 specific people who match this ICP today, by name? If no, the ICP is still too vague.

## The ICP discovery process (7 questions, coach-mode)

Run one question at a time. Lock each answer before moving on. Do not skip ahead.

### Question 1: Who is the LAST customer you said yes to that you regret?

This unlocks the anti-ICP fastest. Resentment is data.

Probe:
- Why did you take the work?
- What about them did not fit?
- What did the engagement teach you about who you should NOT serve?

Note the pattern. This becomes Section 7 (anti-ICP). Most founders can answer this in 30 seconds because the memory is fresh.

If the user has zero customers (true Pre-Rev), skip to Question 2 and revisit Question 1 after the first paid engagement.

### Question 2: Of the customers you said yes to and would say yes to again, what do they share?

Look for 3-5 specific patterns. Push the user past "they pay on time" to:

- Company stage / size
- Role / seniority
- Decision-making power
- The specific situation they were in when they bought
- The specific outcome they hired you for

If the user has zero customers, use proxies:
- Conversations that lit them up
- Discovery calls that converted (even if for someone else's business)
- People they admire in their target market

### Question 3: What is the smallest unit you can serve?

"Companies" is too big. "Departments" might be. "A specific role at a specific stage of company" is usually right.

Why this matters: a smaller unit means a tighter offer, sharper outreach, faster sales cycle, higher close rate. Stable Solo and Scaling Solo founders almost always sell to the smallest viable unit.

Examples:
- "Companies" → too big
- "Sales departments" → still too big
- "First-time Sales Managers at B2B SaaS" → right size
- "First-time Sales Managers <18mo in role at B2B SaaS 50-500" → even sharper

### Question 4: Where are they today, specifically?

Get firmographic. Use precise ranges:

- Industry: (e.g., "B2B SaaS", not "tech")
- Company stage: (e.g., "Seed to Series B")
- Employee count: (e.g., "50 to 500")
- Revenue: (e.g., "$5M to $50M ARR") (if known)
- Geography: (e.g., "US + Canada + UK") (if relevant)
- Funding status: (e.g., "Venture-backed" or "Bootstrapped": pick one)

Vague answer ("growth-stage companies") gets pushed back. Specific answer ("Series A to Series C, 50-500 headcount") locks.

### Question 5: What is their job to be done?

What outcome are they hiring you for? Not the deliverable. The outcome.

- Deliverable: "A 90-day coaching engagement"
- Outcome: "Confidence in their first 6 months as a manager and a plan they can run without a coach"

Probe:
- What would they tell their boss when they bought this?
- What would they tell their spouse?
- What does success look like for them in 90 days? 1 year?

Lock the outcome in their words. Use it in positioning, copy, and outreach.

### Question 6: What is the buying trigger?

Why now? Not why ever. Buyers buy on triggers. Without a trigger, the conversation stays in "interesting" forever.

Common buying triggers for B2B services:
- New role (just promoted, on probation)
- Missed quota / missed goal
- Lost a major client / employee / partner
- Funded (new budget, pressure to grow)
- Boss pressure (improve or out)
- Internal review failed (bad performance review, 360 feedback)
- Public failure (visible miss)
- Personal stake (kid born, life event, identity shift)
- Competitor moved (industry shift, new pressure)

Pick the 1-2 triggers that fire the strongest buyers. Outreach copy leads with the trigger.

### Question 7: What does the anti-ICP look like?

Now the inverse. Who would you refuse, even if they offered to pay?

This is not theoretical. Anti-ICP is operational. It informs:
- Who you refuse to take a call with
- Who you politely decline after discovery
- Who your outreach explicitly does NOT target
- Who you redirect to a peer

Anti-ICP categories to define:
- Stage mismatch ("Solopreneurs with no team", "Enterprise CEOs")
- Role mismatch ("VPs of Sales", "Founders without a sales team")
- Situation mismatch ("Companies in active fundraise")
- Pain mismatch ("Just looking for a thinking partner, not real change")
- Trigger mismatch ("Aspirational shoppers without a forcing function")

If the anti-ICP feels harsh, that is the right depth. Founders who try to serve everyone serve no one.

## The ICP output card

After all 7 questions lock, produce the ICP card:

```
ICP: [Business Name]
v0.1.0, [Date]

STAGE
- Industry: [specific]
- Company stage: [specific]
- Employee count: [range]
- Revenue: [range, if known]
- Geography: [list]
- Funding: [venture / bootstrapped / either]

ROLE
- Title(s): [primary + secondary]
- Seniority: [IC / Manager / Director / VP / C-suite]
- Tenure in role: [range, e.g., 0-18 months]
- Decision power: [signs alone / influences / requires buy-in]

SITUATION
- 1-line description of their current state
- Specific moment they would describe to a peer

PAIN
- Surface pain: [what they would Google]
- Real pain: [what they would say at 11pm to their spouse]

JOB TO BE DONE
- Outcome they hired you for, in their words

BUYING TRIGGER
- Primary trigger: [event that fires the buy decision]
- Secondary trigger: [reinforcing event]

ANTI-ICP
- Stage anti-fit: [list]
- Role anti-fit: [list]
- Situation anti-fit: [list]
- Pain anti-fit: [list]
- Trigger anti-fit: [list]

5 NAMED PEOPLE WHO MATCH
1. [Name], [company], [why they match]
2. [Name], [company], [why they match]
3. [Name], [company], [why they match]
4. [Name], [company], [why they match]
5. [Name], [company], [why they match]

NEXT STEPS
1. Brief positioning skill with this ICP card
2. Brief offer-design skill with the job-to-be-done
3. Update CLAUDE.md > Offer + ICP section
4. Use the 5 named people as the first outreach list
```

Save the card to `docs/icp.md` (or wherever the user stores documentation).

## The 5-names test

Every ICP definition must end with 5 specific people the user can name today.

If they cannot name 5, the ICP is still hypothetical. Push back:

> "You described the ICP as 'first-time sales managers at B2B SaaS.' Name 5 specific people, by first and last name, who match this today. If you cannot, the ICP needs sharpening or research."

This is the operational test. It separates definitions from descriptions.

If the user can name 0-1: ICP is too vague. Re-run questions 3-4.
If the user can name 2-3: ICP is partially valid. Use these as your interview targets (see customer interview templates).
If the user can name 5+: ICP locks.

## Customer interview templates (free)

If the user cannot name 5 yet, run customer interviews. The output of interviews refines the ICP.

Interview targets (in order):
1. Past customers who matched well (most valuable, most likely to talk)
2. Past customers who did NOT match well (defines anti-ICP)
3. Prospects who said no (defines objection patterns)
4. Peers in adjacent roles at target ICP companies (defines language patterns)

Free interview channels:
- LinkedIn DM with specific ask ("Can I ask you 5 questions about your first 90 days as a sales manager?")
- Personal network referrals (warm intros)
- Industry communities (Slack, Discord) (ask permission first)
- Substack / newsletter subscribers (if you have one)
- Past clients of complementary services (find someone who serves your ICP at a different layer)

Interview question framework (10 questions, 30 min):

```
1. Tell me about your role and how long you have been in it.
2. What is the hardest part of your day-to-day right now?
3. What is the hardest part of the last 90 days?
4. When you have hit walls before, who have you turned to? Why them?
5. What was the trigger that made you seek help / consider coaching / want change?
6. If you had a magic wand, what would be different in 90 days?
7. What have you tried that did not work? Why did it fail?
8. What did you wish someone had told you when you started this role?
9. If you were going to spend $X on a coach / consultant / tool, what would it have to do for you to say yes?
10. Who else should I talk to who is in a similar spot?
```

Run 5-10 of these. Record (with permission). Look for:
- Common language patterns (the words THEY use, not the words you use about them)
- Common triggers (the moments that fire the buy decision)
- Common objections (what made them say no in the past)
- Common alternatives (what they tried before you)

After 5-10 interviews, the ICP card writes itself. The user is no longer guessing.

## Stage-aware adjustments

### Pre-Rev Builder

ICP is hypothesis. Pure 7-question pass. No real customer data to lean on.

Required depth:
- All 7 questions answered
- 5-names test required (if cannot name 5, the ICP is too speculative)
- Customer interview targets identified (run 5 before locking)
- ICP card saved as v0.1.0 hypothesis

Pre-Rev variant of the ICP card adds a TEST PLAN section:

```
TEST PLAN
- Run 5 customer interviews in [next 2 weeks]
- Test outreach with 20 cold messages to ICP fit
- Re-evaluate ICP card after first 3 discovery calls
- ICP locks at v1.0 when first paid engagement closes
```

### Early Operator (<$5K MRR)

User has 1-5 customers. ICP based on real data, not hypothesis.

Required depth:
- All 7 questions answered
- 5-names test required (and easy at this stage)
- Past customer review: which closed and stuck? Which churned or were bad-fit?
- Anti-ICP based on real regret list

### Stable Solo ($5-25K MRR)

User has 10-30+ customers. ICP refinement, not creation.

Required depth:
- All 7 questions answered
- Customer cohort review: top 20% of revenue, bottom 20% of regret
- Anti-ICP based on documented bad-fit patterns (refunded, churned, complained)
- Sub-segment analysis: are there 2 ICPs masquerading as one?

At this stage, ICP often splits. The skill helps the user decide whether to:
1. Pick the higher-value sub-segment and double down
2. Build two ICPs with two offers (only at $25K+)
3. Stay broad and lose sharpness (almost never the right answer)

### Scaling Solo ($25K+ MRR)

User has 50+ customers. Two-ICP playbook possible.

Required depth:
- Two ICP cards (primary + secondary), distinct enough to require different positioning
- Revenue attribution per ICP
- LTV per ICP
- Acquisition cost per ICP
- Decision: continue both, sunset one, or split into separate offers

## Anti-patterns to refuse

### "I serve everyone"

Push back hard. Universal solutions are universal failures.

> "Everyone is not an ICP. Pick the one buyer who pays the most and complains the least. We can always expand later."

### "Small business owners" / "Entrepreneurs" / "Business leaders"

These are not ICPs. They are markets. Push for specifics.

> "Small business owners covers a Series C SaaS founder and a solo Etsy seller. They do not buy the same way. Which one pays you and shows up?"

### "I do not want to pick because I might miss opportunities"

This is the most common Pre-Rev objection. The truth is the opposite: picking unlocks opportunities because outreach, copy, and positioning sharpen.

> "Niching is not closing doors. It is opening one door wider. You can always add a door later."

### "Anyone serious about [outcome]"

Push back.

> "Anyone serious is not an ICP. Specify by role, stage, and situation."

### Defining an ICP without a buying trigger

Without a trigger, the ICP is theoretical. Push:

> "Why would they buy NOW, not later? Without a trigger, you sell into 'interesting' forever. What is the moment that fires the decision?"

### Skipping anti-ICP

The anti-ICP is half the operational value. Without it, the user takes work they regret.

> "Anti-ICP is not optional. Who would you refuse, even if they offered to pay? That answer protects the 80% of your time you spend serving the right people."

### Borrowing an ICP from a competitor or course

If the user names an ICP that sounds suspiciously like the one from a course they took, push:

> "That ICP belongs to [course / mentor]. What is true about YOUR best customers that may be different?"

## Common traps

### Two ICPs hiding as one

When the user names the ICP, listen for a split. "I work with B2B founders and solo coaches" is two ICPs. They do not buy the same way. They do not pay the same price. They do not have the same trigger.

Pick one. Sunset the other or table it for v2.

### ICP definition built only from past customers (no future buyers)

If the user has 10 past customers and defines the ICP entirely from them, they may be optimizing for the past. Push:

> "Of the 10 customers, who do you wish you had more of? Who do you wish you had fewer of? The ICP should be the 'more of' group, not the average of all."

### ICP definition that does not match the user's energy

If the user defines the ICP as "C-suite enterprise leaders" but their energy lights up when they talk about "first-time managers," the ICP is wrong.

Coach question: "When you describe your work, when does your energy go up? That is your ICP, not the one you think you should sell to."

### Forgetting to update CLAUDE.md

The ICP card in `docs/icp.md` is the long form. CLAUDE.md > Offer + ICP is the working memory. Both must update.

### Confusing buyer with user

In B2B, the buyer is often not the user. Define both:

- Buyer: VP Sales (signs the contract)
- User: First-time Sales Manager (does the coaching engagement)

The ICP card should name both layers if they differ. Positioning may need to address the buyer's frame. Coaching delivery addresses the user.

## ICP versioning

The ICP card has a version. It updates when:

- The user pivots ICP entirely (v2.0)
- The user adds a sub-segment (v1.5)
- The user refines based on customer interview data (v1.1)
- The user prunes the anti-ICP based on new bad-fit patterns (v1.1)

Save old versions at `docs/icp-archive/icp-v0.1.0-2026-Q1.md`. The archive shows the evolution and helps the user notice when their ICP has drifted from reality.
