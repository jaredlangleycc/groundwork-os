## The post

---

Saturday morning. Coffee. Notebook. Kid asleep upstairs. The hour I write best is the hour I almost trade for one more meeting every week. I almost did it again yesterday.

I noticed it because I'd been watching myself all week for the same pattern in other domains.

Three places I was reacting when I thought I was leading.

The first was the morning Slack scroll. I'd told myself it was a "quick check." It was 23 minutes. I came out of it knowing what everyone else needed from me, and not what I needed from the day.

The second was the interrupt one-on-one. A client called Tuesday. We were supposed to talk about her quarterly plan. She had a fire from that morning. I let her tell it, and then I solved it on the spot. She walked out with the fire put out and no plan. She walked back into the next fire by Wednesday.

The third was the 4 pm deal review. Friday afternoon, I was reviewing pipeline. I'd planned the review for Monday morning. By Friday at 4 pm the meeting was about a deal that wasn't urgent at noon and was urgent by 4:45.

The pattern is the same in each.

Something noisy showed up. I responded. The response felt productive because I'd done something. But the day had been hijacked.

The fix isn't a calendar trick. The fix is the pause. Three to five seconds between trigger and response. Long enough to ask: is this the work I planned to do, or the work that just showed up?

Most of the time it's the second one. Sometimes that's correct. Often it's not.

The full essay covers the other two triggers and the 5-day reset: jaredlangley.cc/blog/three-reactive-triggers

What's the one trigger you'd pick? Reply or DM.

## What works in this post

- **First line "Saturday morning. Coffee. Notebook." earns the click.** Specific, sensory, no preamble.
- **Paragraph breaks every 1-2 sentences.** Phone-readable.
- **Three discrete triggers, each named.** Reader can save and act on one.
- **Specific details** (23 minutes, 4:45 pm, three to five seconds) anchor the abstraction.
- **The land sentence "Most of the time it's the second one" is the quote.** Stands alone.
- **The invite is specific ("What's the one trigger you'd pick?")** Reply or DM, not "thoughts?"

## Reach pattern (typical)

For a Langley personal LinkedIn post:

| Hour | Impressions | Engagement |
|---|---|---|
| 0-1 | 200-400 | 5-15 reactions, 0-3 comments |
| 1-6 | 800-1500 | 20-50 reactions, 3-10 comments |
| 6-24 | 1500-3500 | 30-80 reactions, 8-25 comments |
| 24-48 | 3500-6000 | 60-150 reactions, 15-40 comments |
| 48-168 | 6000-12000 | 100-250 reactions, 25-60 comments |

The first-hour bump matters. Reply to every early comment within 30 minutes if possible.

## Reply playbook

When comments come in:

| Comment type | Reply pattern |
|---|---|
| "This resonates" | Specific follow-up: "Which trigger?" Convert vague into named. |
| "I do this too" | Ask what they did about it. Convert into testimonial DM. |
| Disagreement | Engage with the actual claim. Don't soften. |
| Question | Answer directly. If detailed answer, suggest DM. |
| Story share | Read, acknowledge specifically (not "thanks for sharing!"). |
| Sales pitch from another consultant | Ignore or polite acknowledgment, no engagement. |

Goal: 50%+ of comments get a reply in 24 hours.

## DM follow-up

When the post drives a DM:

1. Read the DM's specific context.
2. Reply with one specific question that builds the conversation.
3. If they mention working on this, offer the lead-gen assessment link (lead magnet).
4. If they engage with the lead-gen assessment, offer the free intro call.

Don't pitch in the first DM. The post earned the conversation, not the close.
