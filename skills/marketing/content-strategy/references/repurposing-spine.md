# Repurposing Spine

The spine is the production pattern that lets a solopreneur ship 10 to 15 pieces of content per week from 4 to 6 hours of writing. One essay drives six derivative pieces. Without the spine, the user invents 10 fresh ideas weekly. With the spine, the user mines one piece six ways.

## The principle

Each week the user writes one essay (the spine). The essay is the canonical statement of a single idea. Every other piece that week is a slice, angle, or remix of that idea for a different channel and format. This is how a 4-hour writing block produces 6 pieces of social content plus a newsletter plus a blog post.

The spine pattern only breaks if the user tries to use it without first writing the essay. Producing social before producing the essay produces 6 disconnected posts. The spine requires the essay first.

## The 6 derivatives from one essay

### 1. Newsletter (Sunday send)

The essay becomes the newsletter body. Add a personal frame on top (1-2 paragraphs of what the user is sitting with this week, ideally tied to a moment from the last 7 days). Add a soft CTA at the bottom (one offer, one CTA, no link list).

Length: 500 to 700 words newsletter total, of which 400 to 550 is essay content.

Format spec: see `newsletter-broadcast` skill for full send workflow.

### 2. Blog post (essay home)

The essay lives on the user's site as the canonical version with full SEO treatment, FAQ schema, and AI-SEO answer block.

Length: 1000 to 1500 words for solopreneur range. (the example coaching business target: 1000 to 1500, adjusted May 2026 from 1500-2000.)

Format spec: see `copywriting` skill plus `ai-seo` skill for schema layer.

### 3. LinkedIn long-form post (personal feed)

Pull the strongest 3-paragraph section of the essay. Lead with the most arresting line. End with one question that invites reply.

Length: 1200 to 1800 characters (LinkedIn's "see more" cutoff is around 200 characters, so the first line carries the hook).

Format: paragraph breaks every 1-2 sentences. White space matters on LinkedIn.

### 4. Threads chain (5 to 7 posts)

Convert the essay into 5 to 7 numbered points or scenes. First post is a hook + frame. Last post offers the full essay link.

Format spec: see `social-content` skill > Threads chain section.

### 5. Instagram carousel (8 to 10 slides)

One slide = one idea from the essay. Slide 1 is the hook. Slides 2-8 are the points. Slide 9 is a question or CTA. Slide 10 (optional) is the offer or newsletter signup.

Visual: brand-aligned, text-heavy, readable on a phone in 1 second per slide.

Format spec: see `social-content` skill > IG carousel section.

### 6. Short-form video script (60 to 90 seconds)

Pick the single most counterintuitive claim from the essay. Open with the claim (no preamble). Give one beat (the why, in 30-45 seconds). Close with the takeaway and a soft CTA (newsletter, not offer).

Use for TikTok, IG Reels, LinkedIn Video, Threads video. One script, four destinations.

Format spec: see `social-content` skill > Short-form video section.

### Optional 7th: quote graphic

Pull the one quotable line from the essay. Brand-aligned visual treatment. Post to IG and Threads. Lowest production cost, often highest reach.

## Production order (the spine workflow)

Monday morning (1-2 hours): write the essay first draft.

Tuesday morning (30 minutes): finalize essay, write the LinkedIn long-form pull.

Wednesday morning (30 minutes): draft Threads chain + IG carousel slide copy.

Thursday morning (30 minutes): draft short-form video script + quote graphic line.

Friday morning (30 minutes): schedule everything into the next week's slots.

Total: ~3 hours of focused production for 7 pieces of content + the blog essay.

If the user can't get a 1-2 hour block on Monday, the spine doesn't work. They either need to move it (early Saturday morning works for many parent solopreneurs) or reduce cadence.

## What the spine does NOT do

- Does not eliminate one-off posts. Reactions to news, replies to comments, real-time observations all sit outside the spine. Budget 30 to 60 minutes/week for these.
- Does not replace voice work. Each derivative still gets voice-scanned. The spine is structure, not voice.
- Does not work for image-first creators (photographers, designers). Their spine is a portfolio piece, not an essay. Different pattern.

## When to break the spine

Break the spine for:

- A real-time event (a launch, a client win, a public conversation worth jumping into), write a one-off that week
- A guest post or podcast (different production lift, different week)
- A series essay that needs 2 to 3 weeks of build (the spine still applies, but to the series not a single essay)

Don't break it for:

- A trending topic the user wants to ride (run it through the pillar test first)
- A "this won't take long" idea (it will, and it'll cost the essay)
- A request from a peer or friend ("can you post about X")

## Failure modes

### "I don't have time to write the essay"

The essay IS the time-saving move. Without it, the user spends 6 hours producing 6 disconnected posts. With it, the user spends 1 hour on the essay and 2 hours slicing. If the essay is the bottleneck, the user is in the wrong stage for this cadence. Drop to Early Operator cadence (4 posts/week, monthly newsletter).

### "The essay is too long to fit on LinkedIn"

That's the point. LinkedIn gets a 3-paragraph pull, not the whole essay. The essay lives on the blog. Linked from LinkedIn.

### "I don't want to repeat myself"

The spine is repetition by design. Different channel, different audience, same idea. Most followers see 5 to 10% of the user's posts. Saying the same thing six ways is how the message lands.

### "All my content looks the same"

If after 3 weeks every essay-spine looks identical in structure, the user has confused spine pattern with template pattern. Vary the essay shape (story, framework, list, manifesto, hot take). The 6 derivatives will follow.
