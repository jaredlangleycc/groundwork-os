Hey {first_name},

Saturday morning. Coffee. Notebook. Kid asleep upstairs. The hour I write best is the hour I almost trade for one more meeting every week. I almost did it again yesterday.

I noticed it because I'd been watching myself all week for the same pattern in other domains. Three places I was reacting when I thought I was leading. Here they are.

The first was the morning Slack scroll. I'd told myself it was a "quick check." It was 23 minutes. I came out of it knowing what everyone else needed from me, and not what I needed from the day. The scroll set the agenda. I just executed against it.

The second was the interrupt one-on-one. A client called Tuesday. We were supposed to talk about her quarterly plan. She had a fire from that morning. I let her tell it, and then I solved it on the spot. She walked out with the fire put out and no plan. She walked back into the next fire by Wednesday.

The third was the 4 pm deal review. Friday afternoon, I was reviewing pipeline. I'd planned the review for Monday morning. By Friday at 4 pm the meeting was about a deal that wasn't urgent at noon and was urgent by 4:45. I gave it 30 minutes that should have gone to next week's prep. Monday came. The deal closed. The prep didn't.

The pattern is the same in each. Something noisy showed up. I responded. The response felt productive because I'd done something. But the day had been hijacked.

The fix isn't a calendar trick. The fix is the pause. Three to five seconds between trigger and response. Long enough to ask: is this the work I planned to do, or the work that just showed up?

Most of the time it's the second one. Sometimes that's correct. Often it's not.

Pick one trigger this week. Kill it for five days. Not all three. One. See what shows up in the space.

If the lead-gen assessment helped you spot which pillar this lives in for you (probably Presence, sometimes Performance if the morning scroll has eaten your strategic time), you already know which one to pick.

If you haven't taken it yet, here's the link: [your-domain].com/quiz

Tell me which trigger you picked. I'll send back the replacement habit for that specific one.

Jared

P.S. The book I'm rereading is "The Effective Executive" by Drucker. The chapter on time logging is the only thing I've found that pre-empts the reactive triggers above. Worth a Sunday afternoon read.
