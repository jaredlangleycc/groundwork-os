# Newsletter Anatomy

Beat-by-beat anatomy of a single newsletter issue. Use as a writing scaffold. Each section has voice notes and length targets.

## Anatomy

### 1. Subject line

- Length: 5 to 9 words, 30 to 50 characters
- Voice: state, don't sell
- Test: would a friend who reads your work text this to you with no context?
- Mobile clipping starts at ~30 characters

Example good: "Three reactive triggers I caught myself doing this week"
Example bad: "Newsletter #42, Reactive Leadership Tips!"

### 2. Preheader

- Length: 5 to 12 words, 50 to 80 characters
- Function: continues the subject, doesn't repeat it
- Always set explicitly. If you leave it blank, the email client pulls the first body line and you've leaked your opening.

Example good (paired with subject above): "Pick one to kill before Friday"
Example bad: "Hi {first_name}, hope you're well..."

### 3. Greeting

- Length: 1 line
- Use first name only
- No "Dear", no "Hi there", no "Hello friend"
- Use the user's natural greeting from samples (some users prefer "Hey", some prefer just the first name with no greeting word, some prefer "Morning")

Voice check: if the greeting feels formal, the rest of the letter is going to feel formal. Adjust the greeting first.

### 4. Personal frame

- Length: 1 to 2 paragraphs, 60 to 150 words
- Function: turn the email from a content broadcast into a letter
- Time anchor: tied to a moment from the last 7 days
- Specificity: smell, sound, light, what you saw, what you said, what your kid said

Voice notes:

- Refuse weather reports ("Sunny morning here in Vancouver")
- Refuse generic recap ("This week was busy")
- Refuse promo intros ("Quick reminder my offer is open")
- Specific moment + specific feeling = the frame works

Examples:

> "Yesterday at my kid's soccer game I caught myself answering Slack on the sideline. I closed the app. The game didn't get less stressful. My presence did."

> "Saturday morning. Coffee. Notebook. Kid asleep upstairs. The hour I write best is the hour I almost trade for one more meeting every week. I almost did it again this morning."

> "A client said this on Friday: 'I have to read my reps' Slack to know what they actually mean.' I sat with that for two days. This week's essay grew out of it."

The frame ties to the essay's theme by bridge sentence at the end of the frame. Like a transition in a film. Doesn't need to be obvious.

### 5. The bridge sentence

- Length: 1 sentence
- Function: pivots from personal frame to essay body
- Voice notes: invisible if done well

Examples:

> "Here's what's been sitting with me."

> "Here's the pattern."

> "Here's what the week looked like, and why it mattered."

This sentence is the seam. If you can hear it, rewrite it.

### 6. The essay (main body)

- Length: 400 to 600 words
- Function: the one idea of the week
- Voice: full voice-rules treatment

This is where the user does the actual work of writing. Most weeks, this section is the blog essay pulled in (the Sunday Spine pattern). The blog essay is 1000 to 1500 words; the newsletter strips it to the 400 to 600 strongest words.

Structure within the body:

1. Opening claim (2 to 4 sentences). The thesis.
2. Beat 1 (1 to 2 paragraphs). The first piece of evidence or example.
3. Beat 2 (1 to 2 paragraphs). The second piece.
4. Beat 3 (optional, 1 paragraph). The third, if it adds.
5. Takeaway (2 to 4 sentences). What changes for the reader after reading.

For shorter newsletters (Pre-Rev or Early Operator), 2 beats max.

### 7. The land

- Length: 1 to 2 sentences
- Function: drop the reader from the body into the CTA without a hard break

Examples:

> "Pick one. Kill it this week."

> "The data is sitting on your calendar. Look at it."

> "If you want to talk through your version, here's the door."

The land does the rhetorical work the CTA cannot. The CTA itself can be brief because the land already moved the reader.

### 8. The CTA

- Length: 1 sentence + 1 link
- Function: one specific action
- Voice notes: no urgency theater, no "limited spots," no "act now"

Examples by CTA type:

**Lead magnet:**

> If this lands, take the lead-gen assessment. It's the version of this conversation as a 12-question pulse: [your-domain].com/quiz

**Offer:**

> If you want to work on your version of this, book a free intro call: https://cal.com/langleycoaching/clarity-session, 60 minutes, free, we work on one real thing.

**Conversation:**

> Reply with the trigger you're going to kill this week. I read every one.

**Content:**

> The full essay is here: jaredlangley.cc/blog/three-reactive-triggers

Pick one. Never stack.

### 9. The sign-off

- Length: 1 line
- Use first name only
- No "Best", no "Cheers", no title

For some users a closer line before the name works:

> Slowly,
> Jared

> Yours,
> Jared

If the user doesn't have a natural closer, just the name.

### 10. The P.S. (optional)

- Length: 1 to 2 sentences
- Function: only when it earns its place

When to use:

- A genuine afterthought
- A second small offering for a different reader
- A real personal detail

When to skip:

- Default. Most weeks, no P.S.

Examples of earned P.S.:

> P.S. The book I mentioned is "An Everyone Culture" by Robert Kegan. Worth the read.

> P.S. If you know a manager in their first year, this is the email I would have wanted at month 3. Forward it.

> P.S. I'm in Portland Friday for a workshop. Reply if you're there.

## Length budget

| Section | Target words | Hard cap |
|---|---|---|
| Subject | 5-9 | 9 |
| Preheader | 5-12 | 12 |
| Greeting | 2-3 | 5 |
| Personal frame | 60-150 | 200 |
| Bridge | 1 sentence | 1 sentence |
| Essay body | 400-600 | 700 |
| Land | 1-2 sentences | 3 sentences |
| CTA | 1 sentence + 1 link | 2 sentences + 1 link |
| Sign-off | 2-5 | 5 |
| P.S. | 0-30 | 50 |

**Total word count target: 500. Hard cap: 700.**

Most weeks the user writes 800+ on first draft. Cut.

## Format rules

- Paragraphs are 1 to 3 sentences. Sometimes 1 sentence on its own line for emphasis.
- White space matters. Email is a phone read.
- No bullet lists in the body unless the essay's structure calls for it.
- Bold sparingly. Once or twice per issue.
- Italics for short emphasis only. Not for whole sentences.
- No headers (h1/h2/h3) in the body. Headers turn a letter into a blog post inside an inbox.
- One image at most. Often zero.

## Voice-rules pass

After the draft, before send, run:

1. Em-dash strip
2. Banned phrase strip
3. "X, not Y" antipattern strip
4. AI-tell opener strip
5. Hedge word audit
6. Archetype check
7. Signature phrase injection (where natural)
8. Tone calibration
9. Cadence mirror

Show "VOICE CHECK: clean" or "VOICE CHECK: N violations rewritten" before the user locks the draft.

## Common drafting mistakes

### Front-loading the CTA

Some users put the offer at the top because they're nervous. Reader bails. CTA always near the end after the body has earned the click.

### Writing the body first

Most users have a body idea but no frame. They write the body, then jam a frame on top that doesn't connect. Write the frame first (or write both, then trim the frame to the bit that bridges).

### Trying to teach too much

500 to 600 words on one idea. If you have 3 ideas, you have 3 newsletters.

### Voice drift

A long essay can drift into different cadence than the user's natural voice. Always re-read the rendered output on phone. Phones expose voice drift faster than desktop view.

### Image dependency

Some users open with a banner image every week. Gmail clips it. Drop the image, lead with text. The reader is reading on a 5-inch screen.

## When to break the anatomy

Break the format for:

- A short emergency / announcement email (200 words, no personal frame, single message)
- A milestone / anniversary letter (longer, more reflective, less essay-driven)
- A guest-curated issue (different structure entirely)

Don't break it for:

- "I have multiple things to share this week" (split into two issues)
- "I'm feeling creative today" (the format is the discipline)
- "This idea is too long for the format" (cut, or push to long-form blog)

The anatomy is a constraint that produces quality at speed. Most weeks the format is the right answer.
