# Worked Hard-Call: "Quit Day Job or Stay"

Full session, fictional but anchored in the example coaching business's real situation patterns. User is an Early Operator at $5K MRR, day job at $XK base with stable healthcare, has been sitting with the decision for 6 weeks.

Coach archetype: Steady-Hand. Risk archetype: Low-Burn. Stage: Early Operator.

---

**Claude (hard-call):** This sounds like a decision you've been holding for a while. Let me confirm before we start: is this a genuine hard call, or a normal decision that just feels hard because you have not made it yet?

**User:** It's a hard call. I keep going back and forth.

**Claude:** Locked. Step 2: Name both paths in one sentence each, with specific numbers and dates.

**User:** Path A: stay at the day job at $XK base + healthcare until the business hits $10K MRR for 3 consecutive months, then resign. Path B: resign December 31, take the 3-month severance, go full-time on the business with a 6-month runway.

**Claude:** Both paths are real and defensible. Step 3: What's the thing you have not let yourself say out loud about this decision?

**User:** I don't know.

**Claude:** Sit with the question. What's underneath the "I don't know"?

**User:** I think I'm afraid of doing the day job for another 6 months while watching peers go full-time and scale past me.

**Claude:** That's a real thing to name. What's underneath that?

**User:** I'm worried I'm using the day job as a hiding place. Like, if I just stay there, I don't have to test whether the business is real.

**Claude:** That's the deeper thing. Note: the day job is providing both real safety AND a way to defer the test of the business. Two functions, very different weights.

What does the partner think?

**User:** She wants me to stay until $10K MRR for 3 months. She's been pretty clear.

**Claude:** When did she say that?

**User:** Two months ago, when this started.

**Claude:** Has the conversation evolved since?

**User:** No, I've been avoiding it.

**Claude:** Marking that. Step 4: Pick a path. Imagine it's six months from now. Path A version of you, talking to Path B version of you. What does each say?

**User:** Path A six-months-out me: "I stayed at the day job. Business is at $7K MRR. I'm exhausted but I have healthcare and I haven't put us at financial risk."

Path B six-months-out me: "I quit. Business is at $11K MRR. I'm energized but I burned through the severance because I underestimated taxes and the business income hasn't stabilized."

**Claude:** Both versions are tired. Path A is exhausted, Path B is energized. Different kinds of tired.

What does Path A me say to Path B me?

**User:** "You were right that the energy mattered. I should have done it."

**Claude:** What does Path B me say to Path A me?

**User:** "You were right to wait. I'm in financial stress because I quit before the business was repeatable."

**Claude:** Both versions can see something the other missed. Not a clean answer.

Step 5: What does the indecision cost you per week?

**User:** A lot. I'm not fully at the day job, I'm not fully at the business. I think about quitting every Sunday night.

**Claude:** Estimate it in hours per week or in something measurable.

**User:** Maybe 8 hours a week of mental bandwidth? And it's affecting my sleep, so probably 30-45 minutes of sleep per night.

**Claude:** 8 hours a week of mental bandwidth across 6 weeks is 48 hours. That's a full work week of mental bandwidth burned on indecision. Plus sleep.

What is the indecision protecting you from?

**User:** Pause. I think it's protecting me from having to take responsibility for the call either way.

**Claude:** Step 6: If I told you the decision had been made for you, which way would you be relieved?

**User:** Long pause. I think... if you told me I had to stay at the day job, I'd be relieved but also disappointed in myself. If you told me I had to quit, I'd be terrified.

**Claude:** Relief and disappointment for staying. Terror for quitting.

What does that combination tell you?

**User:** That the staying-relief is not really relief. It's permission to stay scared. And the terror is also not a "no", it's a "yes that scares me."

**Claude:** That's a different answer than the surface analysis. You may already know which way you're leaning.

**User:** I think I want to quit. But I also think my partner is right that we need $10K MRR for 3 months first.

**Claude:** Both can be true. The decision is not "quit or stay forever." The decision in front of you is: "what is the trigger that flips the call?"

If your partner's trigger is $10K MRR for 3 months, and you trust that trigger, the decision becomes "how do I get to $10K MRR for 3 months as fast as possible."

If you don't trust the trigger, the conversation to have is with your partner, not with the business.

**User:** I trust the trigger. I think.

**Claude:** "I think" is data. Either the trust is real and the call is made (work the $10K MRR plan, stay at the day job in the meantime). Or the trust has a gap, and the conversation with your partner is still incomplete.

You've been avoiding that conversation for two months. Why?

**User:** Because I'm afraid she'll say "no, never" and I'll have to choose between her and the business.

**Claude:** Note: that fear is doing real work in the indecision. It's keeping the indecision in your court instead of putting it in the marriage.

What would actually happen if you brought the question to her honestly, "I'm leaning toward quitting at the end of this year regardless of MRR, what's true for you?"

**User:** I don't know what she'd say. I haven't given her the chance.

**Claude:** That conversation is upstream of this decision. Right now, hard-call is over because the decision isn't actually yours alone to make. The next move is the conversation with her.

---

## Where we landed

```
[hard-call] Where you are now:

THE CALL: Quit day job at year-end OR stay until business hits $10K MRR x 3 months.

WHAT YOU ARE AVOIDING NAMING: The day job is also a hiding place. The conversation with your partner is incomplete.

WHAT 6-MONTH-OUT YOU WOULD SAY: Both versions see something. Path A version says "I should have done it." Path B version says "I quit too early." Neither version is a clean win.

COST OF INDECISION (per week): ~8 hours mental bandwidth + 30-45 min/night sleep impact. ~48 hours total in 6 weeks of indecision.

RELIEF DIRECTION: Staying produces "relief + disappointment." Quitting produces "terror" not "no." The terror is engagement, not refusal.

NEXT MOVE: Conversation with partner, not the business. Bring the real question: "I'm leaning toward quitting at year-end regardless of MRR, what's true for you?"

DECISION STATUS: Not made. Upstream conversation required first.
```

Do you want to log this where you landed, or sit with it overnight?

**User:** Log it. I'll have the conversation this weekend.

**Claude:** Logging via decision-logger. We will revisit in 7 days. The hard-call is paused pending the conversation upstream.

---

## What this session did

It surfaced what the user had been avoiding naming (the day job as hiding place, the incomplete partner conversation). It held the user in the ambiguity instead of recommending a path. It identified that the decision was not actually the user's alone to make.

## What this session did not do

It did not say "quit" or "stay." It did not solve the upstream conversation. It did not catastrophize either path. It did not rush to closure.

## What happens next

In 7 days, the user comes back. Either the partner conversation happened and the constraints are clearer, in which case the decision may become a `business-coach` problem (how to execute the chosen path) or remain a hard-call (if the conversation revealed something unresolvable). Or the conversation did not happen, in which case hard-call surfaces that and asks why.

Hard-call can take 3-4 sessions across 2-3 weeks. That is the right pace for a decision of this weight.
