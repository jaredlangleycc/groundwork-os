# Failure Mode Bank

Comprehensive bank of failure modes organized by plan type. Use to seed the pre-mortem when the user's named failure modes are thin. Stay specific. Never use the generic version when a typed version exists.

## Plan type: New offer launch

**Execution failures:**
- Launched without a single proof story or testimonial in the offer page
- Built the delivery system before validating demand
- The minimum-viable version of the offer was too maximum
- Lost momentum between launch announcement and first sale
- Did not have the bandwidth to sell while delivering

**Market failures:**
- ICP overlap with current offer cannibalized existing pipeline
- The offer's positioning was indistinguishable from 3 obvious competitors
- The price point fell into the dead zone (too expensive for impulse, too cheap to be premium)
- The pain you solved was not urgent enough to displace the prospect's current solution
- The buyer was not the user (champion-block dynamic)

**Family-floor failures:**
- Launch week required 50 hours of focused work, ate two weeks of family floor
- Onboarding the first customers required evening availability you did not have
- The recurring delivery requires weekend hours

**Identity failures:**
- You built the offer your peers wanted you to build, not the one you wanted to deliver
- 90 days in, the work felt like an obligation instead of a craft
- The offer required you to be a person you do not want to be (always-on, performance-driven)

## Plan type: First hire

**Execution failures:**
- The weekly work the hire would do was not actually defined when they started
- Onboarding the hire required management time you did not have
- The hire required tools or process you had not built yet
- The hire's work product required quality control time greater than the work they saved

**Market failures:**
- The hire was solving the wrong bottleneck (you hired admin help when sales was the gap)
- The hire's output went directly to the customer and reduced quality

**Risk failures:**
- The monthly cost exceeded the marginal revenue the hire enabled
- The hire was added before the prior month's revenue was repeatable
- The contract was longer than the runway justified
- The hire's onboarding cost (your time) was not budgeted

**Family-floor failures:**
- The hire required nightly Slack response to be effective
- The hire's working hours crossed your protected blocks
- Managing the hire became your evening work

**Identity failures:**
- You hired to feel like a "real founder" and not for a specific bottleneck
- The hire shifted your role from operator to manager and you missed the operator work

## Plan type: Paid advertising launch

**Execution failures:**
- The creative was generic and did not differentiate
- The landing page was not changed to match the ad message
- There was no kill signal defined when the budget was set
- Spend went up before the previous spend's data came in
- Manual review of leads was abandoned after week 2

**Market failures:**
- The targeting was too broad and produced low-intent traffic
- The targeting was too narrow and produced low volume
- The ad platform's audience was not where the ICP actually lives
- The ad got compliance-flagged or rejected and the workaround diluted the message

**Risk failures:**
- Budget kept rising despite no signal of working
- "Just one more month" became 4 months
- Cumulative spend exceeded the cap before the kill signal was named

**Family-floor failures:**
- Daily ad management slipped into evenings
- Lead response time required after-hours availability

## Plan type: Pricing change

**Execution failures:**
- Existing customers were not given a clear grandfather plan
- The new pricing was rolled out without updating proposal templates and contracts
- Sales scripts and follow-up emails referenced old pricing
- The website was changed but the calendar booking page was not

**Market failures:**
- The new price was below the perception threshold for the new positioning
- The new price was above what the current ICP would say yes to
- The new price exposed your offer to a tier of competitor you had been below

**Risk failures:**
- Revenue dropped for 60+ days while the new price found its audience
- The runway was not enough to absorb the drop
- The change was made because of one good month, not a sustained trend

**Identity failures:**
- The price change felt like a betrayal of early customers
- You doubted the new price every time a prospect hesitated
- You started discounting privately, undoing the change

## Plan type: Quit the day job

**Execution failures:**
- Resignation timing did not align with current client contracts
- Healthcare and benefits gap was not budgeted
- Tax withholding shift produced a surprise quarterly bill

**Market failures:**
- The pipeline that justified the move dried up in the first 60 days
- A flagship client churned in month 1 and the business income halved
- The recession or market shift hit timing-poorly

**Risk failures:**
- Runway calculation used best-case revenue, not realistic
- Family expenses were not actually surveyed (lifestyle inflation post-quit)
- Partner's income was assumed stable but was not

**Family-floor failures:**
- "Full-time business" meant longer hours, not better hours
- The expanded availability got eaten by busy-work, not deep work
- The home office space was not built for full-time use

**Identity failures:**
- You missed the structure of the day job more than you expected
- The community/team you had at the day job did not transfer
- "I am a [founder/coach/builder]" felt less stable than expected

## Plan type: New marketing channel

**Execution failures:**
- The cadence required was not actually feasible (daily content claimed, weekly delivered)
- The format required a skill you did not have (video, audio production)
- The platform's algorithm changes in the first 90 days

**Market failures:**
- The ICP does not actually use the platform you are launching on
- The platform's monetization mechanics do not favor your offer type
- Existing competitors have a 5-year head start and own the topic

**Risk failures:**
- Content production cost was underestimated
- The opportunity cost (other channels deprioritized) showed up as flat overall pipeline
- "Build the audience first" deferred revenue past your runway

**Identity failures:**
- The content style required a persona you did not want to maintain
- You started chasing engagement metrics over depth

## Plan type: Course or productized offering

**Execution failures:**
- Content production took 3x the estimated time
- The course platform required tech the user did not have
- Marketing the course was treated as a separate project that never started

**Market failures:**
- The course audience overlapped too much with the 1:1 audience and cannibalized
- The price expected for a course is lower than the user could earn with the same hours on 1:1
- Course completion rates are low, and a course with low completion does not convert to upsells

**Risk failures:**
- The launch did not produce the cohort fill rate assumed
- Refund requests exceeded the budget allowance

## Cross-plan failure modes (always check)

These show up regardless of plan type:

- **The plan compounds with another active bet.** You agreed to two new commits this quarter and either alone would be fine.
- **The plan requires you to be a person you are not currently.** Hustle culture, always-on, performance-driven, public-facing. Identity drift kills more plans than execution.
- **The plan requires partner support that has not been explicitly given.** Implicit support is fragile.
- **The plan has no kill signal.** Without a kill signal, every plan is open-ended runway burn.
- **The plan was made in an emotionally loaded moment.** Post-good-month confidence or post-bad-month panic. Look for sober re-decision.

## How to use this bank

When running the pre-mortem and the user's named failure modes feel thin:

1. Find the plan type that best matches the user's plan
2. Surface the 3-5 most likely failure modes from the typed list
3. Force a "realistic / hypothetical" tag from the user
4. Move on to step 4 (coach archetype calibration) with the realistic ones marked

Never read the full list at the user. Pick 3-5, walk through them, move on.

## Update protocol

When a real plan fails in the wild, add the actual failure mode to the relevant typed list. Real failures > theoretical failures. The bank gets better with use.
