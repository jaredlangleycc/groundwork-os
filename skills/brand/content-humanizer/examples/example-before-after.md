## Example 1: Cold email (B2B SaaS sales manager outreach)

### BEFORE (AI-generated first draft, ~115 words)

> Hi Sarah,
>
> Hope this email finds you well! I noticed you recently transitioned into a sales management role at [their company], congratulations on the new chapter!
>
> I work with B2B SaaS sales managers in their first 18 months on the job to help them avoid the common pitfalls that derail so many first-time leaders. Many of my clients tell me that the hardest part isn't the strategy, it's the constant context-switching between coaching reps, hitting their own number, and managing up.
>
> Would you be open to a quick 30-minute conversation to explore whether this could be valuable for you? I'd love to learn more about your situation and see if there's a fit.
>
> Looking forward to hearing your thoughts!
>
> Jared

### AFTER (scrubbed, ~85 words)

> Hi Sarah,
>
> I noticed you moved into the BDR Manager role at [their company] last quarter.
>
> Most B2B SaaS sales managers I work with hit the same wall in their first 18 months. The strategy is not the problem. The problem is coaching reps, hitting their own number, and managing up. All in the same day.
>
> Open to a 30-minute conversation about how the first 90 days are going? Specific, no pitch.

- "many of my clients tell me that the hardest part isn't the strategy, it's..." → reframed as direct claim (X-not-Y antipattern)

**AI-tell scrubs:**
- Opening "Hope this email finds you well!" deleted
- "Congratulations on the new chapter!" deleted (generic flattery tell)
- "I'd love to learn more" deleted
- "See if there's a fit" deleted (vague hedge)
- Closing "Looking forward to hearing your thoughts!" deleted
- "Many of my clients tell me" → restructured (vague trust signal)

**User-banned:**
- "circle back" not present (clean)
- "moving forward" not present (clean)

**Cadence:**
- 4 sentences shortened for Sharp:4 + Direct:5
- "the common pitfalls that derail so many first-time leaders" → "the same wall" (concrete over abstract)

**Signatures:** not natural fit here; not injected.

**Final pass:**
- Removed 1 instance of "would you be open" → "Open to" (tighter)
- Locked first-person singular throughout (no "we")

## Patterns to notice across all three examples

Common scrubs that appear in every Langley draft:

1. **"In today's fast-paced world" or variants:** ALWAYS deleted entirely. Strongest single AI tell.
2. **Listicle openers ("Here are X reasons"):** ALWAYS rewritten as claim-led openings.
3. **"X, not Y" antipattern:** ALWAYS rewritten affirmatively.
4. **Em-dashes:** ALWAYS split into separate sentences (Operator voice prefers periods over em-dashes).
5. **Rhetorical questions ("Sound familiar?", "Imagine if..."):** ALWAYS rewritten as direct claims or observations.
6. **Hopefulness closers ("Hope this resonates", "Looking forward to your thoughts"):** ALWAYS deleted.
7. **"The truth is..." / "Here's the thing...":** ALWAYS deleted.
8. **Adjective stacks ("powerful, evidence-based, transformative"):** ALWAYS reduced to one or zero adjectives.
9. **First-person plural ("we") in solo content:** ALWAYS switched to "I."
10. **Signature phrases:** Injected once per piece when they fit; never forced.

## What the scrubber should NOT change

These three examples preserve:

- The actual claims (scrubber does not invent or remove substance)
- Direct quotes (Langley does not use quotes in these, but the rule holds)
- Specific facts (90 days, B2B SaaS, 18 months: all kept exact)
- The pronoun choice (I, not we, throughout: matches solo founder identity)
- The signature elements (The flagship offer, Presence/Performance/Wholeness)

The scrubber strips voice violations. It does not edit the message.
