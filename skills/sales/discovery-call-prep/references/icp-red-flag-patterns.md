# ICP Red Flag Patterns

6 red flag types that signal a prospect may not be a fit. Each pattern includes the signal, what it usually means, how to test it on the call, and how to qualify-out gracefully if confirmed.

ICP red flags don't mean the call should be cancelled. They mean the user should listen for specific signals AND be ready to redirect or qualify-out within the first 10-15 minutes if the prospect is clearly outside ICP.

## Pattern 1: Wrong role

The prospect's role doesn't match the user's stated ICP.

### Signal
- ICP: Frontline sales managers <18 mo. Prospect: VP Sales 8 years.
- ICP: Solo founders <$10K MRR. Prospect: Series A CEO with 30 employees.
- ICP: Early-career engineers. Prospect: Engineering Manager.

### What it usually means
- They've taken the offer's positioning broadly and self-identified up the ladder.
- They want adjacent help (coaching, advice) but the specific offer doesn't fit.
- They're testing the offer for a team member, not themselves.

### How to test on the call

Q: "What were you hoping to get out of our time today?"

Listen for:
- "I want to help my team / direct reports." (Signal: they're scoping for someone else)
- "I'm at a different stage but I read your stuff." (Signal: they want consulting, not the offer)
- "I want to compare notes." (Signal: peer-level, not buyer)

### How to qualify-out gracefully

> "Real talk: based on what you've shared, what you need is closer to [executive coaching / advisory work / team coaching, depending on which signal hit]. The flagship offer is built for [original ICP]. I can refer you to [name of person better positioned] for the work you're describing. Want the intro?"

This honors their time and yours. Builds reputation as someone who refers well, not someone who tries to fit a square peg.

## Pattern 2: Wrong company stage

Company size or revenue is materially outside ICP.

### Signal
- ICP: B2B SaaS 50-500 employees. Prospect: 8-person startup or 2,000-person enterprise.
- ICP: $2M-$20M ARR. Prospect: pre-revenue or $100M+ ARR.
- ICP: Bootstrapped. Prospect: Series C with VC pressure.

### What it usually means
- Smaller-than-ICP: prospect can't afford the offer, will haggle on price.
- Larger-than-ICP: the user's framework may be too lightweight, prospect needs enterprise-grade.

### How to test on the call

Q: "Tell me about your team / company right now. Stage, ARR, headcount?"

Listen for:
- Revenue/funding mention that's clearly outside ICP range.
- Decision-process complexity (legal review, procurement, board approval) that doesn't fit solo coaching.

### How to qualify-out

For smaller-than-ICP:
> "Honest answer: at your stage, my work might be too much overhead vs. the higher-leverage things you could spend on. I'd send you to [free resource / cheaper alternative / different coach who specializes in earlier stages]."

For larger-than-ICP:
> "My work is built for [original size range]. At your scale, you probably need [executive coaching / leadership consulting / OD work]. [Name of bigger firm or coach] would be better. Want an intro?"

## Pattern 3: Wrong tenure

Prospect is past the window where the offer is most valuable.

### Signal
- ICP: New managers <18 months in role. Prospect: in role 5+ years.
- ICP: First-time founders Year 1-2. Prospect: Year 7 founder.
- ICP: New parents Month 1-12. Prospect: empty-nesters.

### What it usually means
- They've been doing the role long enough that the foundational work the offer provides is below their level.
- They want refinement / advanced work, which is a different engagement type.

### How to test on the call

Q: "How long have you been in this role / at this stage?"

Listen for:
- Tenure outside ICP window.
- Sophistication signals: they use frameworks, name books they've read, reference past coaches.

### How to qualify-out

> "You're past the 'new manager' window where the flagship offer is designed to deliver the most. What you need now is closer to [advanced leadership coaching / specialty work / peer group]. I can either refer you, or we can scope a different format that fits where you actually are. Which is more useful?"

## Pattern 4: Wrong company type / industry

Industry, business model, or company culture doesn't match ICP.

### Signal
- ICP: B2B SaaS. Prospect: nonprofit, media, e-commerce, agency.
- ICP: Tech. Prospect: traditional services, manufacturing, government.
- ICP: Remote-first. Prospect: in-office traditional culture.

### What it usually means
- The framework's specifics (deal cycle, comp structure, team dynamics) don't map cleanly.
- The user can do the work but will have to do double-duty translating to the prospect's context.

### How to test on the call

Q: "Tell me about how your company operates day-to-day."

Listen for:
- Comp structure references that don't match SaaS norms.
- Team / hierarchy structure that's unusual.
- Industry-specific terminology you don't recognize.

### How to qualify-out

> "Most of the patterns I work with come from B2B SaaS. The work translates, but you'd be doing 30% more translation than my typical client. Two options: A) we still do the work, but I scope it tighter so the translation is contained. B) I refer you to a coach who specializes in your industry. Which fits?"

This isn't an absolute qualify-out. Industry mismatch is sometimes a YES if the prospect is willing to do the translation. It's a flag, not a kill.

## Pattern 5: Wrong intent signals

Profile suggests they're not the buyer for this offer.

### Signal
- Prospect is themselves a coach / consultant / similar service provider.
- Prospect has booked multiple discovery calls with competitors recently.
- Prospect's profile shows they're "exploring" rather than buying.
- They've taken free assessments / read content extensively without converting.

### What it usually means
- They want to compare notes (peer-level).
- They're shopping (will buy from cheapest).
- They're a student of the field (consume, don't pay).

### How to test on the call

Q: "What's your process when you decide to invest in something like this?"

Listen for:
- "I'm just exploring right now."
- "I've talked to [N] other coaches / consultants."
- "I do similar work myself."

### How to qualify-out

For peer-level:
> "I notice we do similar work. Two paths: A) we have a peer conversation and I don't sell you anything. Or B) you actually want to work with me as a client. Either is fine. Which is this?"

For comparison-shopping:
> "If you're at the comparison-shopping phase, I'll save you time: I'm not the cheapest option. The reason people work with me is [specific differentiator]. Worth seeing if that's what you need. Otherwise, here are 2 names I respect who price differently: [referrals]."

For "always exploring":
> "Sounds like you're in research mode, which is great for some seasons. The offer is built for people in 'doing' mode. Want me to send you a free resource and we reconnect when you're in doing mode?"

## Pattern 6: Free-rider signals

They've consumed extensively but never converted, and they may not be a viable buyer.

### Signal
- Downloaded 5+ resources, attended 3+ webinars, never replied to follow-ups.
- Engaged on social but never moved to call until now.
- Mentioned wanting "to support your work" without specifics.

### What it usually means
- They like the content but the offer isn't right for them.
- They have a tight budget and are extracting maximum free value.
- They've been to multiple free things and call cycles without buying.

### How to test on the call

Q: "What got you to book this specific call, after [N] months of [whatever they engaged with]?"

Listen for:
- "I just thought it was time" (vague, not specific reason)
- "I love your content, wanted to meet you" (peer-level)
- "I figured I should finally talk to someone" (not pain-driven)

### How to qualify-out

> "Honest framing: people who book this call after [N] free things either: A) hit a specific pain that made them ready, or B) want to keep extracting free value. No judgment either way. Which is this? If A, tell me the pain. If B, I'm happy to chat for 15 min but I'm not going to sell you anything."

This is direct. Some prospects respect the directness and convert. Others self-select out. Both are wins.

## How red flags should appear in the discovery brief

The brief lists each detected red flag with:

1. Pattern type (1-6)
2. The specific signal observed in research
3. What it likely means
4. The question on the call that will test it
5. The qualify-out language if confirmed

Format:

```
ICP RED FLAGS

→ Pattern 1: Wrong role
Signal: He's been in role 5 years (LinkedIn). ICP is <18 months.
Likely means: Wants advanced work, not Foundation.
Test on call: Q1 + Q3, listen for "I've done [framework] before"
Qualify-out: "What you need is closer to [advanced format]. Let me refer you."

→ Pattern 4: Wrong industry
Signal: Healthcare-tech, not classic B2B SaaS.
Likely means: Some patterns translate, some don't.
Test on call: Q3 (how company operates), listen for industry-specific blockers.
Qualify-out: NOT recommended. Offer the work with tighter scope instead.
```

## When NO red flags fire

Sometimes the prospect is a clean ICP match. The brief notes:

```
ICP RED FLAGS: None detected.
Prospect is a clean ICP match: [name, role, tenure, company stage all in range].
Focus the call on uncovering specific pain, not on qualifying fit.
```

This frees the user to focus on the discovery work, not on assessment.
