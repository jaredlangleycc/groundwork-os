# Morgan Pricing Objection Worked Example

Real prospect: Morgan, Founder of a B2B SaaS startup, flagship-offer proposal stalled past 21 days at Proposal Sent stage. Morgan's pushback in his reply: "I love the work, the price is a stretch right now."

This example walks through how the close-mechanics skill processes the objection, diagnoses the underlying issue, and produces a reframe response.

The actual back-and-forth happened May 7-15, 2026. This document is the skill's output that helped Jared respond.

---

## CLOSE-MECHANICS WORKBOOK: Morgan

### THE OBJECTION

Morgan's reply, verbatim:

> "Jared, love the work. The price is a stretch right now. We just closed a small bridge round and the burn is real. Is there flexibility on the $X,XXX?"

### WHAT IT USUALLY MEANS (3 interpretations)

A) **Value gap.** He doesn't see $X,XXX worth of value yet. Fixable with reframe.

B) **Budget reality.** He genuinely can't afford $X,XXX right now given the bridge round + burn. Different conversation, scope or timing question.

C) **Anchoring.** He's testing if Jared will discount. Some founders ask reflexively. Qualify-out signal.

### DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION

Jared's response (sent May 8):

> "Morgan, fair pushback. The price is the price for a reason, but let me make sure I'm reading you right. When you say 'stretch,' is it: A) the price-for-value math doesn't work yet (we haven't surfaced the right ROI), B) the budget reality regardless of value (cash flow is tight), or C) testing if I'll discount? All three are fair, but the response is different."

Morgan's reply (May 10):

> "Honestly, B. The bridge round was smaller than we hoped. I see the value clearly from our last conversation. I just don't have $X,XXX sitting in the account right now."

### REFRAME (interpretation B confirmed: budget reality)

The skill's reframe template for "budget reality":

> "Got it. Real options:
> A) Wait until budget opens up. I'll send a calendar reminder for [specific later date]. Until then, here's the 1-pager on what we'd cover so you can sit with it.
> B) Phased payment: same $X,XXX total, but stretched. $800 at signing, $800 at day 30, $800 at day 60. Spreads the hit.
> C) Lighter version: 60-day engagement (8 sessions instead of 12) at $1,800. Same framework, shorter window.
> Which fits where you are?"

Jared's actual response (sent May 10):

> "Morgan, got it. Three options, each one is real:
>
> 1) Wait until the budget opens. I'll send a reminder for September 1 (or whatever date you name). Until then, here's the 1-pager we discussed [attached].
>
> 2) Phased payment of the same $X,XXX: $800 at signing, $800 at day 30, $800 at day 60. Same engagement, smoother cash hit.
>
> 3) 60-day version of the engagement: 8 sessions instead of 12, $1,800. Same framework, shorter timeline.
>
> Reply with 1, 2, or 3 and I'll send the matching proposal.
>
> Jared"

### NEXT STEP IF REFRAME LANDS

If Morgan replies with 1, 2, or 3: send matching proposal within 24 hours, advance deal to "Proposal Sent v2" in HubSpot, follow-up cadence in `follow-up-tracker`.

If Morgan replies with a fourth option (e.g., "can we do it for $1,400"): refuse same-scope discount, offer scope-down option 3 again.

### NEXT STEP IF REFRAME DOESN'T LAND

If Morgan goes silent for 7+ days: A/B/C close pattern from `follow-up-tracker` PS1.

If Morgan counters with same-scope discount: walk-away framing.

> "Morgan, I won't drop $X,XXX to $1,400 for the full engagement, because that signals the original price was inflated. The scope-adjusted options (60-day at $1,800, or phased payment) are real options. The same-scope discount isn't on the table. Let me know which option works, or we can close-loop this cleanly."

### WHAT DAVID ACTUALLY REPLIED (May 15)

> "Phased makes the most sense. Send the v2 proposal with the payment plan."

Jared sent the revised proposal same day. Signed May 19. Foundation kickoff scheduled June 2.

### VOICE SCAN

All Jared's responses passed:
- 0 em dashes
- 0 "X, not Y" patterns
- 0 hedge words
- Imperative form ("Reply with 1, 2, or 3" not "you could reply")
- Direct framing ("the same-scope discount isn't on the table") not soft framing

## Lessons from this example for the close-mechanics skill

### 1. The diagnostic question is high-leverage

Asking Morgan "which of three things is true" forced him to name the actual issue. Without the diagnostic, Jared might have offered a generic discount that didn't address the real cash-flow timing issue.

### 2. Same-scope discount was correctly refused

The $X,XXX price is locked. Offering a same-scope $1,400 would have:
- Signaled the original price was inflated
- Set a precedent for future haggling
- Made other clients (who paid full price) feel ripped off if they found out

The scope-adjusted options (60-day at $1,800, phased payment) preserved the per-day rate while accommodating Morgan's situation.

### 3. The phased-payment option saved this deal

For founders mid-funding-cycle, cash flow timing matters more than total cost. The phased payment ($800 × 3 instead of $X,XXX lump) made the deal feasible WITHOUT changing the economic outcome.

This option should be in the proposal-writer skill's default investment section for high-ticket coaching offers.

### 4. The "wait until budget opens" option (Option 1) was real

Even though Morgan didn't choose it, offering "wait" as a real option (with a specific reminder date) signaled Jared wasn't desperate. That signal alone often increases close rates because the prospect feels like they're choosing the engagement, not being sold to.

### 5. The walk-away language was prepared but not needed

If Morgan had counter-offered with a same-scope discount, the walk-away language was ready:
> "The same-scope discount isn't on the table."

That phrase is direct without being harsh. It holds the line. He didn't push because the three options gave him a real path.

### 6. Voice rules held throughout

Morgan's situation tempted toward softer language ("I totally understand," "I really appreciate the question," "Let's see if we can work something out"). The skill stripped all of it. The response sounded like Jared, not like a generic sales rep.
