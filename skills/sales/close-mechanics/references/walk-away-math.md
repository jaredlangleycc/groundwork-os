# Walk-away Math

When to walk away from a deal. The cost of a bad-fit client. How to release cleanly without burning the relationship.

The walk-away move is counter-intuitive for new sellers. They believe every deal should close. Experienced sellers know: some deals shouldn't. Closing bad-fit clients costs more than walking away cleanly.

This reference explains when and how.

## When to walk: the 6 signals

A walk-away decision usually involves 2+ of these signals. Single signals are warnings, not walk-triggers.

### Signal 1: Repeated no-shows or rescheduling

The prospect has rescheduled or no-showed 2+ times.

What it predicts: ongoing chaos. If they can't show up for the sale, they won't show up for the engagement. The "no-show client" pattern is one of the strongest predictors of refund requests and churn.

Example: Prospect rescheduled 3 times. Each reschedule was last-minute. Each new time also didn't work. Walk.

### Signal 2: Won't commit to a decision date when asked directly

The user asked "what's your timeline for deciding" and the prospect won't give a specific date.

What it predicts: indefinite stall. The prospect doesn't want to say no but also won't say yes. The deal is decoration in pipeline, not real.

Example: User: "When will you decide?" Prospect: "I'll get back to you." User: "Specifically, what's the latest?" Prospect: "I don't know, let me think." → walk after 1 more touch.

### Signal 3: Values misalignment

The prospect's stated values clash with the user's framework or values.

What it predicts: a fundamentally bad working relationship. Even if the engagement closes, it'll be friction-heavy. The client will challenge every framework decision, the work won't compound.

Example: Coach uses Wholeness framework that includes family-floor work. Prospect says "I work 80 hrs/week and I'm proud of it, I don't need balance, I need more output." Wrong framework for them. Walk + refer.

### Signal 4: Negotiating on every aspect

The prospect is haggling on price, scope, timing, format, and terms simultaneously.

What it predicts: a difficult client. They'll renegotiate during the engagement. Scope creep + price disputes incoming.

Example: Prospect wants 90-day engagement for the 60-day price, with extra sessions thrown in, payable over 6 months instead of 3. Walk + refer (or politely close-loop).

### Signal 5: Disrespectful of user's time / boundaries

The prospect has crossed a stated boundary. Examples:
- Emailed at 11pm expecting same-day response
- Booked a call during stated unavailable hours
- Used pushy or manipulative language

What it predicts: the boundary violations escalate during the engagement. This is the predictor for the "client from hell" pattern.

Example: Prospect sent 3 emails in 24 hours, last one at 11:47pm Saturday, saying "I need an answer by Monday." Walk + close-loop.

### Signal 6: Math doesn't work for them financially

The prospect's stated financial situation can't actually support the engagement.

What it predicts: refund requests, payment delays, scope arguments. Even if they close, the engagement is high-stress.

Example: Prospect mentions they're 4 months from runway end at current burn, can't justify $X,XXX. Walk politely. Refer them to free resources.

## The cost of NOT walking

Quantify the cost of taking a bad-fit client. Helps the user resist the urge to close every deal.

### Cost type 1: Refund probability

Bad-fit clients refund 40-60% of the time within the refund window. Cost = full engagement fee returned + delivery time invested.

For a $X,XXX flagship engagement:
- Standard refund rate: 5-10%
- Bad-fit refund rate: 40-60%
- Expected cost of bad-fit: $1,200-$1,440 in refunds + 6-10 hours of delivery time

### Cost type 2: Reputation drag

Bad-fit clients who don't refund often complete the engagement unhappy. They:
- Don't refer
- Don't testimonial
- Sometimes leave negative reviews
- Sometimes badmouth in their network

Cost = lost future referrals (a happy client refers 1-2; a bad-fit client refers -0.5).

### Cost type 3: Time on the engagement

Bad-fit clients consume MORE time per session because they're not engaged with the framework. They:
- Resist the homework
- Require more pre-call prep ("how do I get them to do X this week")
- Generate more email volume between sessions

Cost = 1.5-2x the typical client time investment.

### Cost type 4: Opportunity cost

While the user is delivering to a bad-fit client, they're not selling to a good-fit client.

Cost = the next client that didn't happen because the calendar was full.

### Cost type 5: Emotional drag

The user's energy is finite. Bad-fit clients drain energy that should be going into the business, the family floor, or rest.

Cost = harder to do the rest of the work that week. Spillover to family floor.

### Math example

If a bad-fit flagship-offer client costs:
- 50% refund probability × $X,XXX = $1,200 expected refund
- 50% complete-unhappy × ($-500 reputation drag) = $-250
- 1.5x time = +5 hours over typical client
- 1 good-fit client foregone over 90 days = -$X,XXX opportunity cost

Total expected cost of a bad-fit client: roughly $3,000 in expected economic loss + 5 hours of time + emotional drag.

Walking away from a bad-fit prospect = preserving $3,000 in expected value.

## How to walk cleanly

The walk-away is one of the most important skills. Done well, it builds reputation. Done poorly, it burns the relationship.

### Step 1: State the read directly

Don't soften with hedge words ("I'm not sure if..."). State what you see.

> "Honest read: based on what we've discussed, I don't think this is the right work for you right now."

### Step 2: Give the specific reason

Vague walk-aways feel like rejection. Specific reasons feel like respect.

> "The flagship offer is built for [specific ICP]. Your situation is more aligned with [different need]. We could force the fit, but the work wouldn't deliver what you need."

### Step 3: Offer a referral or alternative

This is the move that builds reputation. The walk + referral pattern produces:
- The prospect tells others "Jared was great, he referred me to [name] instead of pushing his thing"
- The referral coach owes you one
- The prospect might come back later when they're a better fit

> "Two referrals: [name 1] specializes in [closer fit]. [Name 2] does similar work at a different price point. Want intros?"

### Step 4: Leave the door open (when appropriate)

For prospects who are just early or have wrong timing:

> "If your situation shifts (you change roles, the budget opens up, the timing changes), the door is open. No follow-up from me, just send me a sentence and we'll restart the conversation."

For prospects who are just bad fit:

> "I'd rather refer you well than push something that won't serve you. The best version of this is you working with [referral] and us staying in touch as peers."

### Step 5: Close the loop in CRM

Update the deal to "Lost" with reason "qualify-out" or "wrong fit" or "referred elsewhere." Don't leave decorative pipeline.

## When NOT to walk

Walking away is the right move when 2+ signals fire. It's the WRONG move when:

### The discomfort is your own, not the prospect's

If the prospect is engaged, fits ICP, has budget, and shows up, but you're nervous about closing, that's a coach-mode conversation about the user's confidence, not a walk-away signal.

### One signal fires but the others are clean

A single reschedule + everything else clean = warning, not walk. Take the call, surface the concern directly ("I noticed we've moved twice, is the timing actually working?"), get the answer.

### The "fit" question is about price not values

If the prospect can afford the engagement but is hesitating on price, that's a value-gap conversation. Reframe (see close-mechanics objection 1). Don't walk.

### The user is using "walk-away" as avoidance

If the user keeps walking away from prospects who push back, the issue isn't the prospect. It's the user's avoidance of selling. Surface this in coach mode.

## The walk-away language bank

10 walk-away phrases voice-scanned for Jared / the example coaching business brand. Adjust to user's voice:

1. "Honest read: this isn't the right work for you right now."
2. "Real talk: based on our conversation, I think [referral] is a better fit."
3. "I could close this deal, but it wouldn't serve you. Let me refer you well instead."
4. "The flagship offer is built for [ICP]. Your situation is closer to [different need]."
5. "I'd rather refer you to someone who fits than force the fit myself."
6. "Three things would need to change for this to work. I don't see any of them changing in the next 90 days. Let me send you to [referral]."
7. "I'm going to do something unusual: I'm going to qualify out of this engagement. Here's why: [reason]. Here's where to go instead: [referral]."
8. "The math of working together doesn't add up for either of us. Let me close-loop this cleanly and send you to [referral]."
9. "If we worked together, you'd be unhappy in 60 days. I can predict it from what you've shared. Let me save us both that conversation. [Referral]."
10. "This isn't a soft no. This is a real no, and I respect you enough to say it directly. [Referral]."

## Tracking walk-aways

Log every walk-away in the CRM with reason. Over time, patterns emerge:

- "I walked away from 6 prospects in Q2 with the same pattern (couldn't commit to decision date)."
- "5 of 7 walk-aways from a specific referral source."
- "3 walk-aways with the same red-flag pattern (mentioned 80-hr week, refused Wholeness framing)."

Pattern recognition makes future qualification faster. The "couldn't commit to decision date" pattern, once recognized, becomes a qualification question in earlier calls.

The pipeline gets healthier the more clean walk-aways you do, not the more deals you close.
