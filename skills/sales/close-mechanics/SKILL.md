# Close Mechanics

The tactical work of moving deals from interested to decided. Objection handling, deal psychology, time-to-close moves. Different from the broader sales coaching: this is what to say RIGHT NOW when a prospect raises a specific blocker, when a deal stalls at a specific stage, when the user is mid-conversation and needs the next sentence.

The skill provides reframes, not scripts. Each response is calibrated to the user's voice and their stated values (no pressure tactics, no manufactured urgency, no manipulation). The work is honest persuasion, where the prospect's best interest and the user's offer align.

Where they don't align, the skill says so. Sometimes the right close is "this isn't the right fit for you, here's a referral."

## When this skill triggers

- "How do I handle 'I need to think about it'?"
- "They said it's too expensive, what do I say?"
- "Deal stalled at proposal stage, what now?"
- "They want a discount, how do I respond?"
- "What's the right way to ask for the close?"
- "They keep delaying, am I being too soft?"
- "Objection: [specific objection], help me respond"
- "How do I test if they're a real buyer or tire-kicker?"

NOT for: writing proposals (use proposal-writer), drafting cold outreach (use cold-email / outreach-drafter), running discovery (use discovery-call-prep).

## The 10 common objections (with reframes)

Each objection includes: what it usually means, how to test the underlying issue, the reframe response in the user's voice, and the next-step plan if the reframe lands or doesn't.

### Objection 1: "It's too expensive"

**What it usually means:**
Three possible meanings, and they require different responses:
- "I don't see the value yet" (most common, fixable)
- "I literally can't afford it" (different conversation about scope or referral)
- "I'm price-shopping you" (qualify-out signal)

**How to test:**

> "Real talk: when you say expensive, do you mean A) you don't see the value yet, B) it's a budget reality regardless of value, or C) you're comparing against someone cheaper?"

Listen for the answer. The reframe depends on which.

**Reframe A (value gap):**

> "Fair. The cost is the cost. The question is: what would have to be true for [outcome] to be worth $X to you? Walk me through your numbers. If the work generates [revenue / time / specific result] and it costs me $X to deliver, the value math is straightforward. If you can't see the math yet, that's a sign we haven't surfaced the right pain on the call."

**Reframe B (budget reality):**

> "That's a different conversation. Two options: A) we wait until you're in a different budget window, no hard feelings, I'll send a calendar reminder for [3-6 months out]. B) we scope a smaller version of the engagement at a lower price. Which fits your situation?"

**Reframe C (price-shopping):**

> "I'm not the cheapest option. The reason people work with me is [specific differentiator]. If price is the primary filter, I'll save you time: here are 2 names who price differently and do good work: [referrals]. The right fit might be one of them, not me."

### Objection 2: "I need to think about it"

**What it usually means:**
- "I'm uncertain about a specific thing and I don't want to look uncertain on the call" (most common)
- "I want to talk to someone else first" (partner, boss)
- "I'm not sure I want to say yes" (real hesitation)

**How to test:**

> "Sure. What specifically are you going to think about? If I can address it now, I will. If it's a real reflection thing, name it so I know what timeline to give you."

The answer reveals which type it is.

**Reframe (specific concern):**
Address the concern directly. Don't move on until they verbally acknowledge it's addressed.

**Reframe (need to consult):**

> "Smart. Two paths to make that easier: A) I can join a 30-min call with [partner / boss] to answer their questions directly. B) I can send a 1-pager built for [their role] that you can forward. Which is more useful?"

**Reframe (real hesitation):**

> "Honest answer: 'think about it' usually means there's a specific concern you're not naming. What's the part of this that's not sitting right?"

This is the directness move. Some prospects will name it. Some won't. If they won't, set a timeline ("how long do you need? 48 hours? 7 days?") and follow up specifically on that day.

### Objection 3: "I need to convince my [partner / boss / co-founder]"

**What it usually means:**
- They've decided yes themselves and just need buy-in.
- They've decided no themselves and they're using the other person as cover.
- They genuinely need a co-decision.

**How to test:**

> "Quick check: if [other person] said yes tonight, are you in? Or are you also still deciding?"

Answer 1: "I'm in, just need to get them on board" = case 1.
Answer 2: "Well, I want to see what they think first" = case 2 (cover).
Answer 3: "We genuinely decide things together" = case 3.

**Reframe (case 1, ready to buy):**

> "Got it. Three options: A) I join a 30-min call with [other person] to answer their questions directly, no extra cost. B) I send you a 1-pager built specifically for [their role / their concerns] that you can forward. C) You forward this proposal as is. Option A converts highest. Option B is fastest. Which fits?"

**Reframe (case 2, cover):**

> "I appreciate the honesty. The 'need to convince partner' is sometimes a polite way to think about it more. No pressure either way. Want to take 7 days and decide, or is there something specific you're working through that I can address now?"

**Reframe (case 3, genuine co-decision):**

> "Respect that. The thing that usually helps in shared decisions is for the other person to hear the same conversation you heard. Want to schedule a 30-min call with both of you? Same questions, same answers, same room."

### Objection 4: "Send me a proposal and I'll review"

**What it usually means:**
- Standard procurement step (legitimate)
- A polite delay tactic
- They want to compare against competitors

**How to test:**

> "Happy to send a proposal. Two questions to make it sharper: A) what would have to be true for you to say yes when you read it? B) who else is part of the decision, and what's the timeline?"

If they answer concretely, it's a real review. If they hedge, it's a delay.

**Reframe (real review):**

> "Sending the proposal Monday. Decision deadline 14 days from then so we both have a real timeline. Anything you want me to make sure is in there beyond what we've discussed?"

**Reframe (delay tactic):**

> "Before I send it, one check: is there a specific thing on your end that needs to change for this to be a yes? If yes, let's address it now. If you're not sure, the proposal won't fix that. We can scope a different conversation instead."

### Objection 5: "I don't have time right now"

**What it usually means:**
- They're genuinely busy (fixable timing)
- They're using busy as an excuse (real hesitation)
- The offer requires more time than they're willing to invest

**How to test:**

> "Two possibilities: A) the timing is genuinely bad, in which case let's scope a future-start window. B) the time commitment of the engagement is too much, which is a scope question. Which is it?"

**Reframe (genuine timing):**

> "Honest answer: most clients tell me they're busy when they sign up, and the engagement helps them be less busy by week 6. If you're booked solid through [specific date], let's plan kickoff for after that. Want me to send a calendar reminder for [specific later date]?"

**Reframe (excuse):**

> "I notice 'no time' is often what people say when something else is the actual blocker. What's the real reason this isn't a yes right now?"

**Reframe (scope issue):**

> "The engagement assumes 60-90 min/week of work between sessions plus the 60-min session. If even that's too much, the engagement isn't the right shape for you right now. Worth scoping a lighter version, or waiting until your calendar opens up?"

### Objection 6: "Can you do it cheaper / discount?"

**What it usually means:**
- They're testing if you'll discount (qualify-out signal).
- They actually have a smaller budget.
- They're comparing to competitors.

**How to test:**

> "Two questions: A) is the issue the price, or the price-for-value? B) are you comparing me to someone specific?"

**Reframe (price-for-value gap):**

> "The price reflects the depth and scope. Adjusting the price requires adjusting the scope. I won't do same-scope-lower-price because that signals the original price was inflated. Two real options: A) reduce engagement length (60 days instead of 90) at $X. B) phased payment (same total spread differently)."

**Reframe (budget reality):**

> "Got it. Real options: A) we wait until your budget window opens (when?). B) you do a lighter version (60-day at $X, or async-only at $X). C) you do something cheaper that's not me, and I refer you to [name]. Which fits?"

**Reframe (competitor comparison):**

> "I'm not the cheapest. The reason to work with me is [specific differentiator]. If price is the primary filter, here are 2 referrals priced differently: [names]. If [differentiator] is what you actually need, the price is the price."

NEVER same-scope discount. The user's pricing rules are locked.

### Objection 7: "We've worked with coaches / consultants before and it didn't work"

**What it usually means:**
- Past bad experience, healthy skepticism
- Bad fit with previous provider, looking for someone different
- They didn't do the work and want to make sure it's different this time

**How to test:**

> "What specifically didn't work? Was it the framework, the chemistry, the implementation, or something else?"

The answer reveals what they're avoiding this time.

**Reframe (framework didn't fit):**

> "Different framework here. I work from [name your framework]. That's either going to resonate or not. Worth seeing if it does."

**Reframe (chemistry issue):**

> "Coaching chemistry is real. The 30-day refund window is exactly for this case. You decide by day 30 if the chemistry is working. If not, full refund. The risk on you is 30 days. The risk on me is delivering well enough that you stay past day 30."

**Reframe (they didn't do the work):**

> "Honest: in my experience, when coaching doesn't work, it's usually that the client didn't do the work between sessions. The engagement assumes 60-90 min/week of homework. If you're committing to that, the work compounds. If not, we'd be repeating the previous experience. Be honest: are you in for the homework?"

### Objection 8: "How do I know this will work for me specifically?"

**What it usually means:**
- They want a guarantee (which you can't give)
- They want proof of past results
- They're genuinely worried about fit

**How to test:**

> "Three possible meanings: A) you want a guarantee of specific results, which I can't give. B) you want to see proof of past results, which I can show. C) you're worried about fit specifically. Which?"

**Reframe (wants guarantee):**

> "Honest answer: I can't guarantee specific outcomes. Anyone who does is lying. What I can guarantee is the work, the framework, and the 30-day refund window. If by day 30 you've decided this isn't aimed at your situation, full refund. That's the only honest 'guarantee' there is."

**Reframe (wants proof):**

> "Fair. The closest analog client to your situation is [name + outcome]. Here's the testimonial: [link]. The reason it might translate to you: [1-sentence parallel]. The reason it might NOT: [honest qualifier]."

**Reframe (fit worry):**

> "We test fit in the first 30 days. The work I do is shaped around [framework]. If it resonates, the engagement holds. If not, you invoke the refund. The risk on you is 30 days, not 90."

### Objection 9: "Why should I work with you instead of [competitor / other coach / DIY]?"

**What it usually means:**
- They're comparison-shopping
- They want to feel like they've done due diligence
- They're testing if you'll talk down competitors (don't)

**How to test:**

> "Tell me what you're looking for from the work. I'll tell you whether I'm the right fit or whether [competitor] is."

**Reframe (genuine comparison):**

> "If [competitor] focuses on [their specialty], they might be a better fit for [specific scenario]. The reason to work with me is [your specific differentiator]. The honest answer is: it depends on what you actually need. Let me ask you 2 questions about what you want from the work, and I'll tell you my honest read on whether I'm the right one."

NEVER trash competitors. Build reputation by being a good referrer.

### Objection 10: "Let me get back to you next month"

**What it usually means:**
- They're punting indefinitely
- They have a legitimate timing block (busy season)
- They're not ready and don't want to say no

**How to test:**

> "Quick check: what specifically changes next month that makes this easier to decide? If you can name it, great. If not, 'next month' is sometimes a polite way to say no."

**Reframe (legitimate timing):**

> "Got it. Specific plan: I'll send a calendar reminder for [specific date next month]. I won't follow up before then. When we reconnect, the conversation picks up where this one left off."

**Reframe (polite no):**

> "Real talk: most 'next month' conversations don't reconnect. If this is actually a no, tell me directly. I'd rather have a clean no than a vague maybe. If you do want to reconnect next month, name the specific date and reason."

## Walk-away mechanics

Sometimes the right move is to walk. The skill helps the user recognize WHEN.

### When to walk:

- 3+ touches at the same stage with no movement
- Prospect won't commit to a decision date when asked directly
- Prospect's stated values misalign with the user's framework
- Prospect is consistently disrespectful of time (no-shows, late, distracted)
- The math doesn't work for the prospect financially

### How to walk:

> "Honest read: based on where we are, I don't think this is the right work for you right now. I could keep pushing, but the better move is to close-loop this conversation cleanly. The door is open if anything shifts on your side. Here's a referral to [name] who might be a better fit for what you actually need."

The walk move builds reputation. It signals confidence, integrity, and respect for the prospect's time.

## Anti-patterns to refuse (the user's values are non-negotiable)

The skill REFUSES tactics that violate the user's stated values:

### "Use scarcity / artificial urgency"

Refuse. "Only 2 spots left this quarter" when it's not true = manipulation. The proposal's 14-day decision deadline is enough urgency.

### "Imply they'll lose status / opportunity if they don't buy"

Refuse. FOMO tactics damage long-term trust.

### "Use loss-aversion framing aggressively"

Refuse. "What's it costing you to NOT do this" once is fair. Five times is pressure.

### "Manipulate via emotional appeals to family / kids / fear"

Refuse. The user works in the leadership coaching space where Wholeness is a pillar. Emotional manipulation contradicts the brand.

### "Quote competitors' weaknesses to position yourself"

Refuse. Builds reputation by being a good referrer. Talks UP competitors when they're a better fit, doesn't talk DOWN to win.

### "Take any client who can pay"

Push back. The user's ICP exists for a reason. Off-ICP clients churn faster, refer less, demand more support time. Sometimes the right answer is to refer out, even when the prospect wants to give you money.

## Stage-aware adjustments

### Pre-Rev Builder

- Most objections are: "I'm not sure this is right for me" (fit) and "I need to think" (delay).
- The user's confidence is lowest at this stage. Hold them to NOT discounting.
- Each closed deal is a high-leverage learning moment. Log outcomes in `decision-logger`.

### Early Operator

- Objections diversify: price, timing, partner buy-in.
- This is the stage where users start hearing "it's too expensive" and want to discount. Refuse.
- Walk-away mechanic becomes critical. Bad-fit clients drain disproportionately at this stage.

### Stable Solo

- Objections shift to: "We've worked with coaches before," "Why you vs [competitor]."
- Reputation is now part of the close. Reference past clients (with permission).
- More walk-aways. Pipeline is healthy enough that the user can be choosier.

### Scaling Solo

- Objections often handled by team (SDR / sales support).
- User handles the final call where the close happens or doesn't.
- Walk-away math is more sophisticated (cost of acquisition vs lifetime value per client type).

## Family floor protection

Close-mechanics work happens on calls and in real-time conversation. Hold the line on family floor:

- Don't take "urgent" closing calls after 4pm if user's floor is 4-7pm
- Don't draft "send tonight" closing emails after the user's daily cutoff
- Don't reply to prospects on Sundays (user's stated no-work day) even if the deal feels urgent

> "Alex's email at 5pm Friday saying 'need to decide by Monday' is creating false urgency. Reply 'happy to discuss Monday morning, calendar here.' Don't pull from family floor to chase a deal that won't close 30% faster anyway."

## Voice-rules integration

Every reframe passes through voice-rules:

- Strip em dashes (use commas, periods, parens)
- Strip "X, not Y" antipattern
- Strip hedge words: just, really, basically, actually
- Strip AI tells
- Match the user's voice samples (rhythm, sentence length)
- Match the coach archetype calibration (Challenger sharper, Reflective gentler)

## Output format

When user asks for help with a specific objection or close moment, output:

```
OBJECTION: [restate what the prospect said]

WHAT IT USUALLY MEANS: [3 possible interpretations]

DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:
[1 question to test which interpretation is true]

REFRAME OPTIONS (3, by interpretation):

A) If [interpretation 1]: [response]
B) If [interpretation 2]: [response]
C) If [interpretation 3]: [response]

NEXT STEP IF REFRAME LANDS:
[Specific action]

NEXT STEP IF REFRAME DOESN'T LAND:
[Walk-away mechanic or escalation]

VOICE CHECK: clean / [violations + rewrites]
```
