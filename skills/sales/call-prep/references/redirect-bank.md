# Call Redirect Bank

30+ phrases organized by call drift type. Use these when the call goes off-topic, when the prospect tries to pivot, or when the user needs to gently steer back to the agenda.

## Drift type 1: Prospect pivots to "tell me about you"

Some prospects ask about the user's background early, often as a delay tactic. Honor briefly, then redirect.

### R1.1: Standard redirect (early in call)

> "Happy to. The 60-second version: [1-sentence credibility statement]. But the better use of our time is YOU. Tell me about [specific prompt from the agenda]."

### R1.2: Senior prospect (CEO / VP)

> "Quick answer: [1 sentence]. The detailed version is on my LinkedIn. What I'd rather use this time for: [specific question tied to their challenge]."

### R1.3: They keep digging

> "I get the impulse. The work I do speaks for itself when we talk about YOUR situation. Pivoting back: [open question]."

## Drift type 2: Prospect asks "how much does this cost" too early

Pricing too early kills the call. Defer without dodging.

### R2.1: Standard

> "Real answer: depends on what we agree we're solving. Let's spend 20 min on that and the price conversation will be clearer for both of us."

### R2.2: They insist on a number

> "Ballpark: the engagement runs [range]. Specific number tied to specific scope. Want me to walk you through what the scope choices look like?"

### R2.3: They're price-shopping (you can tell)

> "If you're at the comparison-shopping phase, I'll save you time: I'm not the cheapest option. The reason people work with me is [specific differentiator]. Worth seeing if that's what you need."

## Drift type 3: "Have you worked with [my type of company]?"

Tempting to answer with examples. Better: redirect to their specifics.

### R3.1: Yes, you have

> "Yes, [name 1-2 clients]. But the more useful frame is whether what I do solves what YOU'RE wrestling with. Let me ask you another question."

### R3.2: No, you haven't (exactly that type)

> "Not exactly your stage, but the adjacent work is [name 1 example]. The reason it translates: [1-sentence reason]. Worth digging into your specific situation to test the fit."

### R3.3: They want logo validation

> "I get wanting reference points. The reference I'd give you is [name + 1-sentence outcome]. The other reference is what we surface in the next 20 min about whether this is your problem to solve."

## Drift type 4: Prospect goes long on context (their company history, org chart, etc.)

Let them set context, then surface the question.

### R4.1: After 5 min of background

> "This is helpful context. Let me ask the question I'd ask anyway: of everything you've described, which part is keeping you up at night right now?"

### R4.2: They keep adding detail

> "Pause for a sec. I have what I need. Here's what I'm hearing as the actual problem: [restate 1-sentence]. Am I close?"

### R4.3: They're not naming the pain

> "Take a breath. What's the part you haven't told me yet? The part you're embarrassed to admit."

## Drift type 5: They challenge your method / approach mid-call

When a prospect pushes back on the framework, that's signal, not threat.

### R5.1: Honest pushback

> "Fair pushback. The reason the framework holds up: [1 specific example]. The reason it might not fit YOUR situation: [1 honest qualifier]. Want to test it on your case?"

### R5.2: Aggressive pushback

> "You're skeptical. Good. I'd rather have you skeptical now than disappointed later. Tell me what's not landing."

### R5.3: They're testing you

> "I notice the questions you're asking are designed to see if I cave. I don't. The work is the work. The question for both of us: is this the right work for what you need?"

## Drift type 6: They get vulnerable / emotional

A prospect opening up about their real pain is a moment. Don't break it with logistics.

### R6.1: They share something hard

> "Thank you for naming that. Stay with it for a second. What's the part you haven't told yourself yet?"

### R6.2: They tear up or get quiet

> [Silence. 10-15 seconds. Do not fill it.]
>
> [Then: "Take your time. We can sit with that."]

### R6.3: They apologize for the emotion

> "No apology needed. The reason this work matters is exactly this. Whenever you're ready, here's where I'd go next: [specific next question]."

## Drift type 7: They try to delay the close

Common late in the call. Standard delay tactics: "let me think about it", "send me a proposal and I'll review", "let me run this by my partner."

### R7.1: "Let me think about it"

> "Sure. What specifically are you going to think about? If I can address it now, I will. If you need 48 hours, name what you'll decide between."

### R7.2: "Send me a proposal"

> "Happy to. To make the proposal sharp, two things: A) what's the criteria you'd use to say yes? B) who else needs to see it for the decision to happen? Send me sentences on each and I'll have it over by [day]."

### R7.3: "Let me run this by [partner/boss]"

> "Smart. Two options to make that easier: A) I can send you a 1-pager built for [their role] that you can forward. B) I can join the next conversation with them. Which is more useful?"

## Drift type 8: Existing client wants to expand scope mid-call

For client check-ins where the conversation wanders into "can you also do X?"

### R8.1: Scope creep request

> "Interesting. Before I say yes or no, let me ask: how does that fit with [original scope]? Are we adding, or replacing?"

### R8.2: They want it bundled in

> "Real answer: that's outside the current scope. I can: A) do it inside the current engagement at no extra cost if we drop [something else]. B) add it as a separate engagement at [price]. C) decline and refer you to someone better fit for that specific thing. Which?"

### R8.3: They're testing the boundary

> "I'd rather over-deliver inside the lines we agreed on than under-deliver across more lines. Let's stay on [original scope] for the next 30 days and revisit at our check-in."

## Drift type 9: The call has zero energy / they're disengaged

Sometimes you can feel the call is dead. Honor it, redirect or release.

### R9.1: Mid-call energy drop

> "Honest check: this conversation feels like it's not landing for you. Either I'm not asking the right questions, or this isn't the right time. Which is it?"

### R9.2: They seem like they're going through motions

> "I notice we're 25 min in and the energy is flat. Two options: A) I'm boring you, in which case let's restart with what you actually want to talk about. B) The topic isn't right for today, in which case let's end early and you give me 5 min back. Either is fine."

### R9.3: Distracted prospect (kid in background, laptop open elsewhere)

> "Sounds like there's a lot going on. Want to grab a different 30 min later this week when you can be present? I'd rather have you fully here for 30 min than half-here for 60."

## Drift type 10: They want to skip to "what would it cost / what's the next step"

Some prospects want to bypass the discovery and jump to the close. Tempting to oblige. Don't.

### R10.1: They want the pitch in minute 5

> "I could pitch you. But the pitch would be generic if I don't understand your situation first. Give me 15 more min on your context, and the pitch I deliver will actually fit."

### R10.2: They're qualifying you out of patience

> "Got it. Quick frame: the engagement is [headline + price]. If that's a non-starter, let's not waste each other's time. If it's in range, the next 20 min is how we figure out if it's the right fit. Which path?"

### R10.3: They're testing if you'll skip discovery

> "Watch what I'm doing here. I'm NOT pitching you yet because I haven't earned the right to. That's what the next 20 min is for. After that, if there's a pitch, it's a real one, not a generic one."

## Voice rules applied across all redirects

Every redirect above is voice-scanned:

- No em dashes
- No "X, not Y" antipattern (rewrite affirmatively)
- No hedge words: just, really, basically, actually, simply, literally, honestly, frankly
- No banned closes
- No filler: "I just want to say", "I appreciate that"
- Imperative form, not "you should"

When in doubt, the right redirect is the shortest one that names the dynamic and offers a clear next step.
